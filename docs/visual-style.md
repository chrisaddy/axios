# Axios Visual Style Bible

Working title: Axios
Primary visual direction: Iconographic Painterly 2.5D

## Visual thesis

Axios should look like a living sacred city rather than a generic fantasy world. The art should draw from Byzantine iconography, late antique architecture, Mediterranean light, wall painting, mosaic, manuscript illumination, and historical street life. The result should feel reverent, human, and explorable.

The visual target is not photorealism, grimdark antiquity, or cartoon nostalgia. It is a stylized historical world where sacred beauty and ordinary struggle coexist.

## One-sentence style statement

Axios is a painterly 2.5D historical adventure with iconographic influence, using luminous sacred interiors, stylized late antique architecture, and human-scale environmental storytelling to evoke Constantinople as both lived city and spiritual world.

## Emotional targets

The player should feel:
- curiosity when entering a new district
- reverence inside sacred spaces
- warmth in moments of mercy and community
- tension in spaces of rumor, poverty, injustice, and political unease
- wonder during feast light, chant, procession, and ritual
- intimacy with households, courtyards, workspaces, and neighborhood life

## Core visual principles

1. City first, spectacle second
   - The city itself is the main marvel.
   - Beauty should come from place, atmosphere, and ritual rather than flashy effects.

2. Sacred and ordinary in contrast
   - The game should contrast narrow dusty streets with radiant interiors, market noise with chant, poverty with ceremonial richness.
   - This contrast is central to the emotional grammar of the world.

3. Stylization over realism
   - Use simplified forms, painterly surfaces, and intentional color design.
   - Avoid detail for detail's sake.

4. Readability over ornament overload
   - Byzantine inspiration should guide the world, but should not drown playability.
   - Keep silhouettes, pathways, interactables, and NPC roles easy to read.

5. Light as meaning
   - Light should not be treated as only technical illumination.
   - It should signal mood, sanctity, danger, transition, and memory.

## Camera and world presentation

Recommended camera:
- 3/4 top-down or gently elevated oblique camera
- enough angle to feel architectural space
- stable exploration framing for readability
- optional close framing for key dialogue, prayer, feast, or cutscene beats

Camera goals:
- clear pathing and interaction readability
- enough depth to make churches, markets, and courtyards feel spatially distinct
- support for painterly scene composition

Do not optimize for:
- twitch combat readability
- cinematic over-rotation
- free camera complexity in MVP

## Rendering approach

Recommended rendering target:
- 3D world with stylized materials and painterly textures, presented from a mostly fixed camera
- or layered 2.5D scenes if that proves easier in engine

Desired qualities:
- hand-painted or hand-guided texture language
- restrained geometry complexity
- warm shadow colors
- soft atmospheric haze in key spaces
- limited but intentional animation in cloth, flame, water, incense, and crowd motion

Avoid:
- physically realistic material obsession
- high-frequency noisy textures
- procedural clutter with no meaning
- overuse of bloom or post-processing glow

## Color script

### Base palette
Use a restrained Mediterranean-late antique base:
- limewashed white
- warm limestone
- pale sand
- terracotta
- muted olive
- sea blue
- dusty rose
- weathered wood brown
- oxidized bronze green
- faded plum

### Sacred accent palette
Reserve richer color for liturgical or focal contexts:
- gold leaf amber
- candle flame honey
- lapis blue
- imperial crimson
- deep wine purple
- icon green
- polished bronze

### Color usage rules
- Most streets and houses should sit in muted, breathable tones.
- Sacred spaces should use richer contrast, but selectively.
- Gold should feel precious and compositional, not everywhere at once.
- Poverty should not be rendered as visual ugliness alone; there should still be dignity and beauty.
- Feast scenes may temporarily intensify saturation and warmth.

## Material language

The world should feel built from a small number of recurring, believable materials:
- plaster and limewash
- stone and marble
- timber
- woven cloth and dyed textiles
- bronze and iron fittings
- oil lamps and candles
- parchment and wax
- ceramic vessels
- mosaic glass and tesserae

Material rules:
- emphasize wear, age, and touch rather than decay pornography
- surfaces should feel inhabited and maintained
- sacred materials should be cleaner, brighter, and more rhythmically arranged
- domestic spaces should contain useful objects with narrative meaning

## Lighting bible

### Exterior daylight
- warm Mediterranean sun
- readable long shadows
- dusty reflected light off stone and plaster
- sea air brightness in harbor-facing areas

### Exterior dusk
- amber sky tones
- lengthened shadows
- lamps beginning to glow
- heightened emotional warmth and anticipation

### Sacred interior
- candle and lamp pools
- shafts of filtered light
- gold and polished surfaces catching light selectively
- gentle haze suggesting incense and still air

### Tension spaces
- cooler reflected light
- tighter shadows
- more visual occlusion
- reduced visual comfort without turning horror-coded

Lighting rules:
- every space should have a clear emotional light identity
- do not flatten scenes with uniform global brightness
- sacred scenes should glow through contrast and composition, not post-process gimmicks

## Environment design rules

District environments should feel handcrafted and socially legible.

Each space should communicate:
- who lives or works here
- what time of day it is
- what social class it reflects
- whether it is public, private, sacred, or ambiguous
- what the player can do here

Environment categories for MVP:
- church courtyard and interior threshold
- market street
- residential courtyard/alley
- harbor edge or forum edge
- charitable house / widow dwelling

Environment composition rules:
- every area needs one strong focal shape
- every area needs clear walkable routes
- every area should have 1-3 interaction hotspots visible on entry
- clutter should tell story, not block play
- verticality should support mood and layering, not platforming complexity

## Character art rules

Characters should be stylized, dignified, and readable.

Silhouette priorities:
- clergy distinct from merchants
- workers distinct from wealthy citizens
- widows, catechumens, monastics, officials, and children readable by posture and clothing
- saints and spiritually important figures recognizable without cartoon halo shorthand in all cases

Clothing vocabulary:
- tunics
- cloaks
- veils
- belts
- sandals
- aprons and work wraps
- clerical garments
- layered cloth indicating class, role, and occasion

Character design rules:
- avoid anime proportions
- avoid fantasy armor language
- avoid exaggerated caricature unless for very minor ambient NPCs
- hands, posture, and gesture matter as much as faces
- portrait art can carry additional emotional nuance during dialogue

## Facial and portrait style

Portraits should be:
- painterly
- expressive but restrained
- rooted in icon/fresco sensibility more than comic-book exaggeration
- emotionally legible in conversation

Portrait usage:
- key NPCs should have dialogue portraits
- portraits may simplify background detail to emphasize face, cloth, and light
- saints and mentors may receive the most carefully composed portrait treatment

## Animation language

Animation should prioritize intention over quantity.

Important animated elements:
- walking cycles with clear personality
- head turns and greeting gestures
- prayer posture and bowing
- cloth drift
- lamp flicker
- incense drift
- water shimmer
- marketplace ambient motion

Avoid:
- overly bouncy cartoon idles
- action-game anticipation poses everywhere
- frantic ambient animation that breaks contemplative tone

## FX and atmosphere

Use effects sparingly and meaningfully:
- dust motes in sunlight
- incense haze in sacred interiors
- sea shimmer near harbor areas
- occasional birds or cloth banners for air movement
- feast lantern glow
- subtle pollen or ash-like particles only when justified

Do not use:
- fantasy particle spam
- magical aura clichés unless tied to very deliberate narrative moments
- excessive lens flare or bloom

## User interface style

The UI should feel archival, liturgical, and cartographic.

Visual references:
- manuscript margins
- annotated maps
- codices
- wax seals
- carved borders
- restrained ornament

UI surfaces:
- parchment, ink, red rubric accents, muted gold accents
- clean typography with strong legibility
- ornament used at section breaks, headers, and important state changes

UI rules:
- function first, ornament second
- no generic fantasy RPG chrome
- formation progress should feel symbolic and elegant, not like neon stat bars
- maps should feel like drawn district fragments, not satellite minimaps

## Interaction readability

Gameplay clarity rules:
- interactable objects should be distinguishable through placement, light, motion, and subtle highlights
- quest-relevant NPCs should stand out through silhouette and staging rather than giant icons whenever possible
- pathways and entrances should read within 2 seconds of entering a scene
- sacred spaces should invite slower observation without confusing navigation

## Audio-visual relationship

Visual scenes should be composed with sound in mind:
- market visuals paired with layered voices, tools, animals, footsteps
- harbor visuals paired with gulls, ropes, water, workers
- church visuals paired with chant, silence, reverb, lamps, cloth movement
- homes paired with close, intimate domestic sound

The world should feel acoustically different from zone to zone.

## Production guidance

For an indie-first slice:
- prefer style consistency over asset count
- create reusable modular architecture sets with hand-authored variation
- establish one palette and one lighting pass per district mood
- use portraits and scripted scene composition to amplify emotional depth without requiring huge animation budgets

Priority asset sets for MVP:
1. church courtyard + interior threshold kit
2. market street kit
3. residential alley/courtyard kit
4. harbor/forum edge kit
5. domestic charitable-house kit
6. core NPC clothing silhouettes
7. dialogue portrait template
8. UI codex/journal template

## Anti-reference list

Axios should not look like:
- a generic fantasy RPG
- a dark souls Byzantine mod
- an anime JRPG
- a flat mobile educational game
- a museum reconstruction with no soul
- a kitschy wall of gold and crosses
- a muddy brown “ancient world” filter

## Final art-direction test

A scene is on target if it feels like:
- a real place someone lives in
- a sacred world where beauty is meaningful
- a city full of social difference and spiritual gravity
- an explorable game space with clear readable purpose
- historical without being trapped by realism
